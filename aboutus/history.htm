About Us Team History About the Site Past Competitions Community Outreach Awards Fundraising

      McKinley High School's initial involvement in FIRST can be attributed to one man: Mr. Art Kimura. Mr. Kimura, a former science teacher at McKinley, a candidate for the first "Teachers in Space" program, and currently an employee of the Hawaii Space Grant Consortium, opened the door for Robotics to flourish in Hawaii. He did this by speaking with Mr. Mark Leon, the NASA Ames Director of Education. Mr. Leon had already introduced FIRST to Hawaii with the sponsorship of Waialua High School (Team 359), but was persuaded by Mr. Kimura to obtain one more grant to allow McKinley High School to become Team 368. Team 254, the Cheesy Poofs, were flown in, having just won the Silicon Valley Regional, by Mr. Kimura to give a presentation on the world of Robotics to both McKinley and Waialua.

      At the same time, McKinley's Electric Vehicle Team had just competed in their fourth Electron Marathon. The Electron Marathon was an electric vehicle competition where students committed themselves to a problem-solving activity. Students built and raced a car of their own design. In addition to building this vehicle, students were required to provide written documentation, give an oral presentation, and participate in an hour-long vehicle endurance race. Under the direction of the Electronics instructor Milton Lau, McKinley successfully won three out of four state Electron Marathon competitions. However, after watching the presentation by the Cheesy Poofs, McKinley made the difficult decision to end their involvement in the Electron Marathon.

      To make a long story short, Mr. Kimura dined with Mr. Mark Leon, the NASA Ames Director of Education, at a neighbor island space-related conference. Mr. Leon informed Mr. Kimura that NASA was introducing the FIRST program to Hawaii beginning with the sponsorship of one team - Waialua High School Team #359. Mr. Kimura inquired if there was funding for an additional school. With a little persuasion, Mr. Leon approved another grant for Hawaii's public school system. Unaware of Mr. Kimura's actions, McKinley became Team #368. Mr. Leon then flew in members of Team #254, Cheesy-Poofs, to visit Waialua and McKinley High Schools.

      Upon retiring from the competition, McKinley donated their winning electric vehicle to Hawaiian Electric Company, the major sponsor of the Electron Marathon, with the intent that the vehicle be used as an engineering platform for schools new to the competition. This marked the end of an era, but the beginning of a new challenge. FIRST called to the students with a great and unlimited potential for growth and learning, and the newly formed Team 368 answered back with determination, commitment, and an unending enthusiasm.

      Upon retiring from the competition, McKinley donated their winning electric vehicle to Hawaiian Electric Company, the major sponsor of the Electron Marathon, with the intent that the vehicle be used as an engineering platform for neophyte schools. The experience gained by the members of the McKinley team encouraged their interest in the field of engineering as many continued their post secondary education at MIT, Carnegie Mellon, and UCLA.

      As the years went on, however, FIRST was simply not enough. Outside of the FIRST season, there was a large gap in the year with no challenges to work on and overcome. The students needed more opportunities to get hands-on experience in order to sharpen their problem-solving skills. In the Summer of 2006, McKinley decided to create their own game using VEX starter kits as the platform. With the summer over, another game was devised in the Fall of 2006 for new recruits.

      In the Fall of 2007, VEX began its meteoric rise in Hawaii with the Hawaii FRC Rookie VEX Tournament. With the inaugural Hawaii FIRST Robotics Competition just a few months away, McKinley helped to spread VEX to the new FRC teams as a way to prepare them for the FIRST season. Finally, VEX introduced their own game and McKinley was fortunate enough to be one of the first locations to debut the game. With this first competition, VEX grew exponentially within local schools, and continues to grow even now.

      McKinley has also contributed to the growth of Robotics programs in Hawaii by holding various demonstrations in the school and within the community, a few examples being McKinley's annual Mini Math Competition, Tiger Showcase Night, the Children's Discovery Center, the Board of the National Science Foundation, the Board of Pacific Resources for Education and Learning, and the State Capitol.

      McKinley strives not only to bring Robotics to our own students, but also to those in the greater community by continuing to volunteer and encouraging others to volunteer as well, with the belief that as long as the Tiger Spirit remains alive in our school, that we will continue to make an impact for years to come.
